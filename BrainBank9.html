Most people recognize reading as a worthwhile habit, but because it can be a time consuming commitment, it's difficult to stick with. 

Here's 3 tricks I've used to read more:

1. Books on the shitter

  • Idk about you but I spend 15-20 minutes on The Johnathon each day. 
  • Keep a book in the bathroom, don't bring your phone, & (if you're bowel movements are anything like mine) you'll be reading 15-20 minutes/day. 

2. Audiobooks at the gym/in the car/in the shower

  • Some people get all snobby and say audiobooks aren't the same. It's a lie. 
  • You're consuming the same information and audiobooks make it easy to kill 2 birds with one stone. 
  • Take caution with the shower one though, I'm currently phoneless because my phone was eaten by the shower monster.
3. Speed read
  • If you understand some basics about sensory motor skills and comprehension you'll be able to increase your reading time by at least double. 
  • This 9 minute video lays out the "science" of speed reading and exactly how to train yourself to get good at it. 

If you aren't a reader at all but you'd like to be, I suggest you start with  Atomic Habits by James Clear . 

His book is all about habit science and is very actionable and practical. You can implement his research to create your reading habit.

ðŸ’¡  How to Innovate (In as little words as possible)


Fundamental steps of innovation (from  Something Really New  by Denis Hauptly)

1st: Understand the reason people use a product or do something (What's the biological human desire people are trying to satisfy?)

2nd: Lay out the steps it takes for them to get the job done

3rd: Once understood from inception to completion start removing steps until the most simple process is reached


Example: 

Humans have a desire for social connection. 

Before social media you needed to physically go out and meet up with someone to connect with them, and it wasn't always instant.

Now all you need to do is go on social media, comment on some posts, like a few things, get liked, etc. & it satisfies that deeper human desire to connect with others. 


"Take a human desire, preferably one that's been around for a really long time... identify that desire, then use modern technology to take out steps.''

ðŸŽ° 4 Cognitive Biases That Increase User Motivation


1. The Scarcity Effect 

  • the appearance of scarcity increases user perception of value 
  • If a product starts off as scarce then becomes abundant; the perceived value of the product goes down

2. The Framing Effect

  • The perceived value of a product is heavily influenced by how it's framed. Ex. location, presentation, price 
  • In a study done at CalTech to see if price had any influence on perception they presented users with samples of wine & told them the price ranged from $5-90. 
  • They measured their pleasure sensors in the brain w/an fMRI while they told them the price. 
  • The higher priced wines spiked higher pleasure. However the samples were the same wine.

3. The Anchoring Effect

  • People often anchor to ONE piece of information when making a decision. Ex. on sale vs. not on sale 
  • People may make their decision entirely based on that 1 anchor, even though it's completely irrational and might not make sense logistically. 
  • If people want to see "on sale" to feel like they got a deal, upping the original price of a product by 20% then slashing it back down with a 20% off sale is a common practice. 

4. The Endowed Progress Effect

  • A phenomenon that increases motivation as users believe they are nearing a goal 
  • Ex. LinkedIn using a" profile strength" meter from 0% to 100% to motivate users to provide more information.
  • Always start users out with progress already made.
