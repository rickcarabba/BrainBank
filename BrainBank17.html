    What's Meditation? (in as little word as possible) 

    Meditation, in its simplest form, is a mind training exercise.

    In Buddhism, you train your mind to detach from human sensation (sex, possessions, cravings) to achieve a "superhuman" state that frees your mind from suffering. 

    That state is called "Nirvana" & translates to "enlightened one"

    & yes, Kurt Cobain named his band after this superhuman state.

    Reaching Nirvana is the goal of the Buddhist meditation practice. 


    As a modern health practice, meditation is used to train your mind to improve focus & control your thoughts and emotions. 


    How to Meditate

    Simply put: you focus on 1 thing, your "grounding point", for a set period of time. 

    When your mind wanders or random thoughts pop up, notice it, then bring your attention back to your grounding point. 

    That can be considered 1 "rep" for your mind training.

    My grounding point is the word "Nature". 


    What works for me, step by step, as simple as can be.

    1. I sit down (sometimes lay), close my eyes, & set a 15-20 minute timer. 

    2. I focus on 1 thing, the word "nature"

    3. I say it in my head & align the syllables with my breathing.

    4. Breath in through my nose (first syllable "Na-" ), breath out through my nose (second syllable "-ture"). 

    5. Repeat. If my mind starts wondering, I gently bring my attention back to my grounding point. Na-ture. 


    Note: This specific type of meditation, using a mantra (nature) & breathing, is called transcendental meditation. I've found it to be the most effective.

     

    That's all meditation is. 

    It seems marginal & easy but you'll be surprised at how difficult it is just to say a word for 15 minutes.

    You're going to get distracted, I do all the time. It doesn't mean you're bad at it. 

    Just notice that your mind wandered and bring you attention back to your grounding point. 

    Doing this repeatedly is what builds the focus muscle.   


    What I Use Meditation for

    I use meditation for 15-20 minutes before high concentration activities & before bed to shut off my monkey mind. 

    My most high focus "between the ears" activity is writing. I need a clear mind to get into flow & stay focused so I'll meditate for 15-20 minutes before I write.

    At night I have issues turning my brain off to fall asleep at a normal hour, so I use meditation to help me wind down & fall asleep. 

    💲 The Psychology of Deals

    As humans, we love hunting for deals. We talk about deals, we buy good deals, & we share deals with our friends. 

    However, a lot of the time, we didn't need the product that was on sale in the first place.

    Our desire for finding deals can be explained by a type of variable reward called "rewards of the hunt". 

    It's the biological desire (& thrill) of acquiring things that aid our survival. 

    It's the part of our brains operating system that developed when we started chasing down wild game to feed our tribes. 

    Now, the pursuit of hunting down wild game has been replaced with the thrill of hunting for deals (among other things). 

    What's more interesting is that the "hunt" itself has been found to drive us more than if we just had what we needed in the first place.

    We're wired to enjoy the pursuit.

    It's why some people enjoy "the chase" in relationships.  They enjoy chasing after each other & earning their partner continuously. 

    Or else they get bored. 

    Conversely, it's why many people play "hard to get" in relationships.


    Marketers and behavioral economists leverage this biological desire to drive consumer purchasing behavior. 


    How Do Companies Simulate Deals? 

    Daniel Kahneman, author of Thinking, Fast and Slow , won the Nobel Prize in Economics for his work on prospect theory. 

    Prospect theory says that people make decisions in relative terms and not absolute terms. 

    What does that mean?

    People are more likely to buy a $100 priced sweater that's marked down to $40 than the same sweater, priced at $45 & marked down to $35.

    Kahneman's research shows that people were more likely to buy the one that looked like a better deal relative to its reference point (retail price) & not the better deal in absolute terms (cheaper overall) even though they were the same product.

    Companies use higher reference points (retail price) to make people feel like they got a better deal, but most of the time it's the same few factories making all the products with a similar COGS. 

    Another prong of prospect theory is diminishing sensitivity.

    The idea that the same change has a smaller impact the further it is from the reference point.

    For example:
    If you buy a remote controller that's $22, & the clerk tells you that they have one on sale for $12 at a location 25 minutes away, would you go to the other location or stay there? 

    In another situation, you're at that same store but now you're buying a new TV that's $550. The clerk tells you that another location 25 minutes away has the TV for $540. Would you go to the other location or just buy there? 

    In relative terms, $22 down to $12 is greater than $550 down to $540. 

    In absolute terms, It's the same 25 minute drive & same $10 saved.

    Kahneman's research shows that the people getting the controller at $12 versus $22 are more likely to go to the other location to get the deal than the people being offered the TV at $540 vs. $550. 

    People make purchasing decisions in relative terms, not absolute terms.

    Using Availability to Create a Deal
    Another factor that makes a deal seem valuable is its availability.

    Restricting availability through scarcity & exclusivity makes a deal seem more valuable.
      
    Many companies do this artificially when there's no real need for a restriction.

    In one study at The Journal of Consumer Research, quantity purchase limits increased sales by 50% (“ Framing the deal: The Role of Restrictions in Accentuating Deal Value ”)

    So when you see a limit to how much you can buy, it could be a psychological trick to increase a product's sales.
