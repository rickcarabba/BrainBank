    Apple Music

How do our thoughts shape our behavior? & can we engineer them to work for us? 

In the pursuit of answers I read books like What to Say When We Talk to Ourselves, The Power of Positive Thinking, & The Defining Decade. 

One theme kept popping up: s ubconscious narratives.

Subconscious narratives are the stories about ourselves that loop silently in our minds w/o us knowing it.

The proverbial TV left on in the backgroundðŸ“º

These "thought loops" often originate during formative experiences in childhood.


A common formative experience:

A student, Jeffrey McJohnson (that's a common name, no?), raises his hand to ask a question in 2nd grade.

After he asks his question, the class laughs at Jeffrey b/c it was a "dumb question."

Jeffrey felt embarrassed & hesitated to ask questions for the rest of his life. 


The resulting subconscious narrative looks like this:

Jeffrey: *thinks of question*

Jeffrey's Mind: "no that's dumb don't ask that"

Jeffrey: *never asks question, keeps thoughts to himself* 

Subliminally thinks: "if my thoughts are dumb, then I must be dumb." 


Poor Jeffrey ðŸ˜¥

Our brain forms this narrative to avoid future pain b/c of the negative experience we had, it's primitive & biological. 

But it doesn't serve us well outside of remembering not to eat poisonous berries.

What's crazy is that, most of the time, Jeffrey doesn't know he has this loop playing in the background. 

Yet it's constantly influencing his behaviour & beliefs.

We often think thoughts are spontaneous things but in reality we have similar patterns that ebb & flow based on certain triggers.

Only if you slap them on paper will you start to notice patterns & code in new intentional thinking. 


Slap your narratives on paper

There's only 1 way I know of doing this, it looks like this: 

Step 1

  • Isolate yourself for a day

Step 2

  • Write down every thought that comes in your mind, in real time.
Step 3
  • As thoughts pop up, ask "why", then "why" again. Write the first answers that pop up.

After a day, you'll have a dozen pages of "thought dialog."

This is the transcript of your "mental radio station", the  tangible data of your thoughts.

Soon, you'll start to see patterns emerging.

Highlight recurring thoughts, notice what thought/thing triggered each one.

Tally  them, cross them out, identify triggers, & rewrite them.


Rewriting subconscious narratives: 

Affirmations & manifestations are a common diagnosis, but to me they seem hollow.

To quote clinical psychologist Meg Jay, "The only way to change is through new conversations & experiences."

Experiences that prove the opposite. Something you can point to.

In Jeffrey's case: If you don't feel like your thoughts are smart, a sk, " What would a person who thought the opposite do?"

Try...

- Learning a new skill

- Attending a conference in a space you're interested in

- Applying to the board of a local commision that makes important decisions

- Answering forums that reward your input from the community.


Then, once you do these things, you'll have things to point at that detach your old thought loop.

When that old thought loop pops up, it won't have any legs to stand on because the concrete experiences you point at will render older narratives irrelevant.
