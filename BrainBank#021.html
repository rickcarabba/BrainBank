"The standard pace is for chumps," said Kimo Williams, after proposing that Derek Sivers complete his Berklee College of Music degree in 2 years.

After finishing at 20 years old, Derek confirmed, "the system is designed so anyone can keep up. If you're more driven than most people, you can do more than anyone expects."

The same goes for language learning.

It's no secret that the language learning curriculum in the US is broken. But just how broken? & is there a better alternative? 

After ~2 months of living in Argentina, 10+ hours/day of experimentation, more than 200 hours of conversation exchanges, & trying every single language service imaginable; I believe there's a better framework.

In the 3 total months of experimenting to get to this framework, I was able to score B2 on the SIELE Spanish fluency exam. Which is 1 level shy of "C1", which represents complete fluency. 

I believe you can achieve complete fluency in 4 months (full time)  by following the framework I outline below.

This is what I wish I had when I first touched down in Argentina, January 2, 2020. 

Note: if you want to geek out over all the experimentation that led to this framework, I wrote a long-form article on it here. 


The Framework

Many of the resources I'm covering were completed pre-covid times, to compensate I've listed the online equivalents for post-covid times. 

I highly recommend relocation (when safe), you can move to a popular South American city for 4 months & keep total expenses under 6K. More on that here.

1. Native 1v1 Grammar Lessons (2-3 hours/day)

  • Need native speaker & 1v1 to move at the best pace. 
  • You'll be able to cover all the grammar, tenses, & subjunctive in 45-60 days.
  • Transition to constructive conversation after to use the tools you just learned in real time. 
  • Use Baselang: It's $149/month for unlimited online 1v1 Spanish lessons. If you practice "full-time" it'd be less than $1USD/hour. 
  • They have all the learning materials you'll need.
2. Social Immersion/Conversation Exchange (3+ hours/day)
  • This is where the magic happens. Make friends that don't speak English then hang out with them almost every day.
  • Go to bars, play a sport, play video games, go to conversation exchanges, find your niche hobbies using Meetup. 
  • Do whatever you normally like doing, but in Spanish.
  • If you can't relocate, use conversations with friends at Baselang or use Meetup to find Spanish conversation groups in your city. 
  • To find local Spanish friends (especially in non-Spanish cities) use Tandem & HelloTalk. 
3. Quizlet Learn (30 minutes/day)
  • Everyday you're going to come across words & phrases you don't know. Add them to a daily quizlet then review the previous day's vocab for 30 minutes every morning.
  • They say 80% of spoken language is made up of the 1000 most common words. Here's my Quizlet with the 1000 most common Spanish words. 
  • It's the Pareto principle applied to languages. 
  • Use the Quizlet "learn" function to cement the vocab in deep. 

During social immersion, ask every person 3 things:

1. To correct your Spanish every time they notice something is off (this will create the fastest possible feedback loop). 

2. To repeat themselves every time you don't understand something (this will train your ear to hear quick Spanish & recognize pronunciation).

3. To get their Whatsapp to stay in touch with them (maybe one day they'll be visiting your native country!)


There it is: roughly 6 hours per day for 4 months.

I'm not gonna sugar-coat it: you're going to be uncomfortable every single day.

The key is speaking Spanish every day from day 1, speak English as little as possible.

You're going to fumble constantly, just keep speaking & getting feedback in real time.

Follow this framework like a full-time job & you'll be fluent in 4 months.

Quick note on finances

Many associate long term world travel with being expensive. Let me offer some benchmarks to defog the lense. 

In Argentina, a 20 minute uber was $2-3, a steak at my favorite parrilla (steak house) was ~$4, an apartment in center city w/ a balcony was $600 (on airbnb).

Argentina's just 1 example but there's a lot of countries that fit the bill (you'll be able to compare "cost of living" on NomadList below).


Resources

Here are the best resources I've come across to support your future adventures.

Housing

CouchSurfing

  • Sometimes completely free, a "travelers helping travelers" site. Very affordable stays, sometimes literally on a couch.
  • Hosts are peer reviewed so you'll vet people with ratings from previous guests.
  • Not sure on the covid protocols tbh, haven't used it since pre-covid.

Homestay

  • Budget friendly (from $5/night in Buenos Aires) stays with a native host to show you around the city. 
  • Kinda like a host family.

Airbnb (special discount deal)

  • The big 'un. 
  • Ask any Airbnb for a "special discount" and half the time they'll give you ~20% off right off the bat.


Meeting

HelloTalk

  • Talk & meet with natives from any city around the world.
  • There's a feed of posts from other language learners (it's like social media for language learning).

Tandem

  • Find conversation exchange partners by language, location, age, etc. 
  • Great way to meet friends.

Meetup

  • Find niche clubs by hobby or activity in any city you go to. 
  • I used it to find pickup soccer in Buenos Aires.

MundoLingo

  • Organized social events at local restaurants for internationals to practice languages. 
  • Show up, put your native countries flag sticker (along with any other languages you speak fluently) on the top left of your shirt then the sticker of the languages you're practicing below that. 
  • Start talking with people that have the opposite of you.


Reading

In Light of Yesterday by Brady Raanes

  • Great primer on the backstory of the global economy, country by country.
  • Free with kindle unlimited.

Vagabonding by Rolf Potts

  • Subtitled "the uncommon guide to the art of long term world travel"
  • Just beautiful & succinct, very practical & perspective changing.


Exploring

Nomadlist

  • This is probably the best resource of them all, they have a remote job board, community of millions of other remote workers, & a phenomenal rating system for filtering cities by safety, internet, fun, cost etc. 
  • Here's their rating of Buenos Aires.


Working

RemoteOK

  • Job board for finding remote jobs


Banking

Capital One Venture Rewards Card

  • No international fee credit card 
  • When I got it they ran a promo that's $500 back when you spend $3000 in 3 months. Pay all your expenses on here then get $500 back.
  • Other bonus: they pay for your Global Entry or TSA PreCheck card ($100 value). I.e. skip the lines at airports ðŸ˜‰

Passbook

  • No international fee banking.
