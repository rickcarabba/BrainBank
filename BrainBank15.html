🤒 Echinacea Flower to Prevent Common Colds


The Covid Fairy came to visit me last week— weirdest symptom was the sound of rice krispies crackling in my ears. 

Why they don't tell you this, idk. 

I digress. 


What's Echinacea Flower? 

Echinacea Flower offers a natural alternative to treating the common cold compared to antibiotics and vaccines.

It's not just voodoo magic either. 

Dr. Craig Coleman's meta analysis at UConn shows that Echinacea ingestion at first onset of symptoms reduces the odds of getting the cold by 58% and shortens the cold duration by an average of 1.4 days. 

The words "at first onset" are key.


How I use It

I keep a bottle of echinacea flower capsules handy for the first instance I feel a potential symptom.

If I feel even an INKLING of sniffle I pop one of these suckers to hump the virus into submission.

I've shared this with multiple friends & roommates the past few years & they've all been pleasantly surprised at how well it works. 

I rarely get a cold. 

🗣  3 Ways to Get People Talking About Your Venture


1. Add Shock Value

Did you know a glass ball bounces higher than a rubber one? 

...reads one fact underneath a Snapple bottle. 


Give people a "pleasant surprise". It's a form of entertainment that our brains enjoy.

When we're surprised we want other people to share in this delightful sensation. 

"Did you know that...[pleasant surprise your product gave people]?"

Along the way we talk about the place, product, company that gave us the pleasant surprise. 


Put another way, break a pattern ppl expect. 

Does this make people ask "IS THIS TRUE?"

If you saw someone in a pink giraffe suit handing out flyers in center city for an online marketing course, would you tell someone what you saw?

If you saw a phone case that was dropped from the top of the Empire State Building and didn't break, would that make you say "holy shit!" ? 


Examples of shock value

  • The Blair Witch Project
  • Black toilet paper
  • Snapple facts
  • Derek Sivers' CD Baby Email (My favorite)


2. Use game mechanics to convey social status

Game mechanics boost WOM because people like showing off their achievements but along the way talk about where they achieved them. 

Strava, the social running app, does this well. 


When I break the mile record at my local park (Mondauk) on Strava I earn the status of "local hero". 

All the other Strava runners see the Mondauk mile leaderboard when they go, and they see my name at the top in relation to others. 

Rick "the local hero" Carabba, has a nice ring eh? 

Now here I am sharing a status they gave me that makes me feel good. & at the same time sharing Strava. 

Engineering ways that people can affirm positive identities about themselves will increase the spread of your product.


Good games do the following: 

  • Give discrete markers that tell players how well they are doing  in relation to others.
  • Give tangible evidence of progress that makes us feel accomplished towards a goal. 
  • Convey status. When status reaffirms a positive identity about ones self we are motivated to share. 


Examples of game mechanics

  • Airlines frequent flyer programs
  • The AMEX "Black Card"
  • Strava titles

3. Make people feel like insiders

I'm gonna let you in on a secret.

There's a bar, in London, that only a handful of people know about. 

It's hidden underground & there's only 1 way to find it. 

Enter an old whiskey bar called Milroy's of Soho on 3 Greek St.

Go in, maybe order a drink, look around a bit. 

In the back there's a small lounge surrounded by bookcases.

Go up to the shelf on the far right, notice there's a book that doesn't quite match the others. 

It's titled "The Vault".

Put a little weight behind the book & watch as the bookcase creeks open to the mysterious sound of a beautiful piano.

You look back at the Milroy's bartender & he sends you off with a wink 😉


People don't keep your secrets, they tell their most trusted contacts. 

But if something's a "secret" the value of the information skyrockets. 

The feeling of having something that not everyone has access to, or better, the feeling of letting other people know that you are a person that knows things others don't, drives our desire to share. 


Make people feel like insiders by: 

  • Limiting the availability of your product
  • Creating a barrier to entry (secret, waiting list, password)
  • Making it referral only (need to know someone to get in)


Examples: 

  • Early access product releases
  • Limited availability "drop" culture
  • "speakeasy" type bars
