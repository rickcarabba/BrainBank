Do you check stuff, every day?

In high school, parties were on the weekends. 

In college, you can find a party every night of the week.

Social media (& the news) are kinda like this. 

There's always something to see. 

Do you party every day? 

Or do you save it for the weekends?


Opportunity Cost of Checking Stuff

The rewards of social come from connecting with people.

But what's the cost to get those rewards? 

Some concrete numbers: 

In a conservative example, say you spend 1 hour/day on social & the marketplace values your time at $35/hour. 

Are the rewards worth $245/week? $980/month? $11,760/year?

Maybe. That's different for each person. 

But it poses a question, "Is there a way to get the same rewards at a discount?"


80/20 Social

What's the 20% of activities that yield 80% of the rewards?

The answer's are different for everyone, for me it's IG stories.

Very easy to niche, the more niche the deeper the conversation and higher likelihood of a rewarding connection.

The next question becomes: 

" Can you get those same rewards by spending only an hour/week (opposed to 7)?"

For me, I've deemed the rewards from social worth about an hour of time currency. 

That's my social budget. 

What I do: 

The platform is slippery, so I delete the apps instead of mustering up the willpower not to check. 

Then, ideally, I redownload for 30 minutes & post my top few things/week. 

Then, the next day, I spend 30 minutes responding to the people that message me.

This is called batching, & it's pretty hard, I slip up a lot.

But after a week or two you experience this cool thing called JOMO.


The Joy of Missing Out (JOMO)

JOMO, coined by Jason Fried (founder of Basecamp ),  is the opposite of  FOMO.

Instead of staying in the loop, you stay out of the loop, purposely.

JOMO is the relief of no longer having the urge to check things (like news, social, etc.).

It's the joy of replacing that "checking" time with creative projects. 

Always wanted to write a book? Start a podcast? Read more? Learn an instrument? Start a business? Learn a language?

Most Elevators

Most startup accelerators say an elevator pitch should contain what you do, for who, & why.

Techstars, the popular startup accelerator, promotes this template: 

"For [target customers] that have [problem x], we [what you do!]"

Allan Dib, author of The 1-Page Marketing Plan , promotes this template: 

"You know [that problem]? Well what we do is [some solution]. In fact we [proof of traction, concept, achievement etc.]"

But when you boil it down, there's really 1 thing that matters. 

Did you make them curious?


Toast Elevators

Curiosity drives behavior. When we are curious it becomes an itch to be scratched.

If the dynamic changes from you talking to them asking, after 1 sentence, you've achieved your goal.

Then in the follow up questions you can splurge on the details. When they're red-hot asking you for it.

Instead of the previous elevator frameworks, try asking "How can I make them creatively curious in as little words as possible?"


Consider these examples: 

"Describe your company in 1 sentence." 


Traditional Elevator: 

  • We deliver gourmet food for people who are on tight schedules. 
  • We create funky pop music that makes people happy dance.

Curious Elevator:

  • If Gordon Ramsey rode Usain Bolt to your door to deliver food, that'd be us.
  • If Michael Jackson's legs could sing, they'd sound like us. 


You don't need to say that you create funky pop music that makes people happy  dance, b/c Michael Jackson's singing legs embodies that. 

When you describe your things in this curious way it also travels further. 

They now have a reason to tell someone about Michael Jackson's singing legs or the picture you painted of Gordon Ramsey riding Usain Bolt to deliver food,  because who can forget that?
